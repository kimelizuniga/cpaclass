Week 7 - Intercultural Communication
3 Main Issues at the root of the problem of Intercultural Miscommunication:
  • Language as a barrier: not understanding verbal communication and language
  • Cultural diversity: i.e. age, race, religion
  • Ethnocentrism: evaluation of other cultures according to preconceptions originating in the standards and customs of one's own culture. i.e. North American’s thinking it’s “wrong” that Asian’s eat with chopsticks instead of forks and knives
Intercultural Communication
  • Intercultural communication (IC) requires knowledge and skills. It also requires understanding and empathy.
  • Perspective is critical: have the point of view that communication is better if it is constructive to avoid misunderstandings and breakdowns
  • Effective IC leads to better productivity and teamwork; diversity in workplace is increasing
  • Common barriers: prejudice, assumptions/stereotypes, language
Influence of Culture on Nonverbal Communication
  • Cultures vary on their specific range and use of behaviours
  • Example: In general, Canadians expect a high degree of respect for public and especially for private property and space. Canadians generally have a very strong sense of space (no more or less than an arm’s length); particularly when speaking or dealing with strangers. It is best to carefully observe each person’s degree of comfort with touching and their preference for personal space (i.e. lining up at a bank)
  • The rules vary from province to province, eg: in Quebec people may not need as much personal space, and touching is more common. Typically, people from Quebec greet each other using more physical signs such as hugs and kisses and may offer kisses on the cheek to newcomers after a few encounters.
  • Space is also important to Canadians when driving – i.e. travelling to Greece; India where lanes are not enforced/communicated by law

Week 9 - Self-Marketing Strategies
4 P's of Marketing
  • Product
  • Place
  • Promotion
  • Price
Product - (YOU)
  • You are entering the job market and competing with other candidates
  • What can you do well?
  • How will you get interest?
  • What are your soft skills and hard skills?
  • Your ”packaging” – appearance and CV
  • Personal branding - Branding is your answer to how you can be more sought after than the competition
Place
  • Where will you market yourself?
    • LinkedIn (online networking)
    • Indeed
    • Respond to job postings
    • Career Fairs
  • The broader your search, the more opportunity
    • Are you committed to Kingston? Ontario? Canada?
    • Where will your certifications allow you to work?
Promotion - (Promote Yourself)
  • How will you create interest?
    • Cover letter
    • Resume/CV
    • Portfolio
    • Networking
    • Interview
Price - Salary and Benefits
  • Research
    • Others with same training and experience
    • Average income in geographical location
  • Understand your whole benefits package, not just the amount of money you earn
  • Know your worth – negotiate!
Steps to a Successful Career Campaign
  • Self assessment
  • Determine marketing strategies
  • Create marketing tools
  • Prepare for an interview (practice STAR technique)
  • Handle an interview (use STAR technique irl)
  • Follow up from the interview
  • Evaluate an offer
  • Achieve success on the job (personal brand)
Transferrable Skill Types
  • Functional skills relate to the functions of a task and can be transferred from your education or one career to another (problem solving, supervising, communicating)
  • Self-management skills are the personality strengths you bring to a situation (i.e. efficiency, trust)
  • Work-content skills are based on information you need to be taught or experience (i.e. language, math, safety regulations)
Hard Skills

Hard skills are job specific skills and knowledge required to perform the job, usually acquired over a finite period of time.

Hard skills are job specific skills and knowledge required to perform the job, usually acquired over a finite period of time.

  • Education, training programs, certifications, on the job training
  • Easily defined and quantifiable such as level of education
Soft Skills

Soft skills are the interpersonal “people” skills and they are harder to define

Soft skills are acquired over time and are transferrable skills (travel with you when transition to a new career)

  • Work Ethic
  • Positive Attitude
  • Communication
  • Time Management
  • Critical Thinking
  • Problem-Solving
STAR Formula for Behavioural Interview Questions
  • STAR = S (situation) + T (task) + A (actions) + R (results)
  • Behavioural questions work from the premise that how you behaved in the past is an indicator of how you may behave in the future
STAR as a Response Technique
  • S (Situation) - Where were you when this happened? (e.g. Working at The Bay or in a chemistry lab). When did it happen? (e.g. Last summer)
  • T (Task) - What specific question, problem or challenge were you facing at the moment?
  • A (Actions) - What steps did you take to do a good job? What style did you use? What policies/ procedures/theories, principles did you apply? This should be the longest part of the answer.
  • R (Results) - What happened because you did a good job? What positive impact did your actions have? What problem was avoided? Qualify and quantify your results whenever possible.
